Polling Average


Now, you can't have a working model without a functional polling average, so I'm starting this page in the inner workings of the model's inputs. The polling average among my provincial models covers every poll throughout the term. So my Quebec model, for instance, covers the polls done since the 2022 election, which you can see on the Wikipedia page for the upcoming Quebec election.

*important note, to fix polls from just a couple of months ago from being weighted next to nothing, the W value follows a pattern being multiplied by a higher amount per previous year:
2024 - multiplied by 5
 2023 - multiplied by 10
 2022 - multiplied by 15
 2021 - multiplied by 20

So, getting to how the formula actually works, first you need to plug in all our variables, which are listed here:
W - weighting
R - rating (as determined by 338canada, where an A+ is a 7, going down half a point to below a D- or if a firm is unlisted, where they get a 1)
n - sample size of the poll
d - date (the number of days from the poll to today)
ΣE - error among all the parties
xᵢ - the result of the current poll for a respective party
x̄ - the current average for the same party leading up to that poll

The Model's Math


In terms of calculations within the model, the foundation is based on previous election results; if possible, the last three elections are incorporated into the model, although this isn't always possible due to boundary changes. There's a varying number of swings that are used in the model, but they generally follow a uniform swing method which works like this:
IF([Previous Result]<[Reimbursement Requirement],(Polling Average),[Previous Result]÷[Previous Overall Result]×(Polling Average))×100
To put it into words, the formula looks at whether the party got above the reimbursement requirement (varies by jurisdiction, but generally around 10%), in which case, that result is divided by the respective party's overall result in that jurisdiction (province, territory, country) and then multiplied by the polling average. In cases where a party has not met the reimbursement requirement, their result for the swing is simply input as the current polling average for that respective area. For instance, the NDP and Greens missed the 10% vote share reimbursement requirement in almost every single riding in the 2025 federal election, so their result is replaced with their current polling average, which, if we're calculating the uniform (overall) swing, it'll take the nationwide polling average, or if we're looking at regional swings, it'll take the specific regional polling average.
