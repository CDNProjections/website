Polling Average


Now, you can't have a working model without a functional polling average, so I'm starting this page in the inner workings of the model's inputs. The polling average among my provincial models covers every poll throughout the term. So my Quebec model, for instance, covers the polls done since the 2022 election, which you can see on the Wikipedia page for the upcoming Quebec election.

*important note, to fix polls from just a couple of months ago from being weighted next to nothing, the W value follows a pattern being multiplied by a higher amount per previous year:
2024 - multiplied by 5
 2023 - multiplied by 10
 2022 - multiplied by 15
 2021 - multiplied by 20

So, getting to how the formula actually works, first you need to plug in all our variables, which are listed here:
W - weighting
R - rating (as determined by 338canada, where an A+ is a 7, going down half a point to below a D- or if a firm is unlisted, where they get a 1)
n - sample size of the poll
d - date (the number of days from the poll to today)
ΣE - error among all the parties
xᵢ - the result of the current poll for a respective party
x̄ - the current average for the same party leading up to that poll

The Model's Math


In terms of calculations within the model, the foundation is based on previous election results; if possible, the last three elections are incorporated into the model, although this isn't always possible due to boundary changes. There's a varying number of swings that are used in the model, but they generally follow a uniform swing method which works like this:
IF([Previous Result]<[Reimbursement Requirement],(Polling Average),[Previous Result]÷[Previous Overall Result]×(Polling Average))×100
To put it into words, the formula looks at whether the party got above the reimbursement requirement (varies by jurisdiction, but generally around 10%), in which case, that result is divided by the respective party's overall result in that jurisdiction (province, territory, country) and then multiplied by the polling average. In cases where a party has not met the reimbursement requirement, their result for the swing is simply input as the current polling average for that respective area. For instance, the NDP and Greens missed the 10% vote share reimbursement requirement in almost every single riding in the 2025 federal election, so their result is replaced with their current polling average, which, if we're calculating the uniform (overall) swing, it'll take the nationwide polling average, or if we're looking at regional swings, it'll take the specific regional polling average.

These varying swings and polling averages are applied to the parties in the model, ranging from only 4 in NL and Yukon, to 8 in Alberta and Saskatchewan, and averaged, varying in weight, so that what we have the most polls in (uniform, regional) has the most importance and (barring large polling error) accuracy on the model. The requirements to be included in the model generally require that a party meet at least one of these:
-Have an elected representative for that party (excludes new parties that were created from floor crossings)
-Important influence on riding results, exceeding being folded under the 'Others' banner
-Polled consistently, or at the very least, enough to make a workable polling average
 So, for example, the Republican Party of Alberta recently got added to the model there because it had an important influence on the Olds-Didsbury-Three Hills by-election, and has started polling (although not consistently enough yet to match #3), but New Blue in Ontario (and the Ontario Party for that matter) was never added because they didn't have an elected representative from the last election (just floor crossings), didn't have a significant influence on riding results and never showed up in polling consistently enough to make a large enough impact.

The other important aspect is the candidate swings, which are applied to ridings depending on the candidates or other local factors to create a more realistic outcome. The baseline candidate swing is 100% (this number is what's multiplied after averaging the swings included in the model (see the table for what is included). There is a set of automatic bonuses applied, like if a Mayor is seeking higher office, they'd get a bonus of 25%, making their candidate factor in the riding much more important and giving that party a boost to accommodate for the name recognition and other clout a mayor gets when seeking a parliamentary seat. On top of this, if they're particularly notable, that bonus gets extended to 30%. Another example is if a candidate is already serving as an MLA/MPP/MNA/MHA/MP, they receive an incumbency bonus, typically 10% which is multiplied by any other manual candidate bonus, like being a former mayor (in which case your 25% bonus from before is halved, like anything else you've formally served as). In addition to candidate bonuses, which are sometimes applied to candidates with notoriety, there are also penalties associated with candidates who are controversial or otherwise carry significant "baggage". On top of these purely candidate-based swings, there are other ones applied to certain ridings for more unexpected swings that the larger polls can't catch on to as quickly. A big example of this was in Surrey in the 2025 Federal Election, which saw a big realignment in immigrant voting habits. Now, this was rumoured in other areas like Brampton, but there was no empirical proof from other elections, like provincial or by-elections, which is why it was only applied to Surrey.
